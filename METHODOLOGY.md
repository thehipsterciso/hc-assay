# HC-Assay GRC Study — Survey Research Methodology

**Version:** 1.0.0
**Effective date:** 2026-06-18
**Scope:** Wave 1 (foundation/baseline) and all subsequent waves unless superseded by a versioned revision

This document describes the survey research methodology for the HC-Assay GRC Maturity Benchmarking Study — a practitioner-led, independently funded empirical investigation into the relationship between stated GRC maturity and observed security outcomes in mid-market organizations. It is distinct from the engine methodology described in [docs/METHODOLOGY.md](docs/METHODOLOGY.md), which governs the ML/NLP analysis pipeline. This document governs the human-subject data collection layer.

It is written to withstand hostile review. Every methodological choice made here is explained, every limitation is named, and every place where this study falls short of an ideal design is stated explicitly rather than papered over.

---

## 1. Research Questions

The study is organized around five pre-registered contrarian questions. They are stated as falsifiable claims, not as curiosities, because the study's value depends on its ability to return a clear verdict — supported, contradicted, or indeterminate — not just descriptive statistics.

**RQ1 — Framework inflation.** Do organizations that self-report higher GRC maturity scores exhibit a systematic tendency to select frameworks with broader scope rather than with tighter outcome linkage, and does this selection account for a statistically significant portion of the variance in self-reported maturity?

**RQ2 — Compliance-resilience decoupling.** Does holding one or more formal compliance certifications (SOC 2, ISO 27001, PCI DSS, CMMC, etc.) predict meaningfully lower breach rates, materially shorter incident response times, or better resilience outcomes — controlling for organization size and industry sector?

**RQ3 — Mid-market agility premium.** Do mid-market organizations (250–5,000 employees) demonstrate better agility-adjusted security outcomes than large enterprises, after controlling for absolute resource levels?

**RQ4 — CISO reporting line as failure predictor.** Does the CISO reporting to a non-CxO function (e.g., IT/CTO rather than CEO, COO, or Board) predict materially worse GRC program performance, independent of budget size?

**RQ5 — Risk quantification gap.** Do organizations that use dollar-quantified risk analysis (FAIR or equivalent) report materially different resource allocation decisions and outcome profiles compared to organizations that use qualitative risk ratings only?

These questions were selected because the practitioner literature treats the answers as settled. They are not settled. Existing benchmark studies do not test them rigorously because the sponsors of those studies have commercial interests in affirming the frameworks those certifications validate. This study has no such interest.

## 2. Survey Design Philosophy

### 2.1 What we ask and why

The survey instrument is designed to measure revealed behavior and documented outcomes, not self-assessed maturity. The distinction matters: asking an organization whether it has "mature governance" is not a measurement; it is an invitation to perform. The HC-Assay instrument asks instead about specific, observable artifacts and decisions that mature governance produces — or fails to produce.

Every question maps to a hypothesis. There are no questions included for completeness, comparability to prior surveys, or sponsor interest. If a question does not contribute to a pre-registered hypothesis, it is not in the instrument.

### 2.2 What we deliberately do not ask

We do not ask respondents to rate their own maturity. We do not ask which frameworks they follow as an endorsement mechanism. We do not ask about vendor product usage. We do not ask questions whose answers could identify the respondent's organization to a third party. We do not collect NPS-style satisfaction scores.

Maturity ratings produced by the instrument are a by-product of scoring specific behavioral and outcome signals — they are not generated by asking respondents how mature they think they are.

### 2.3 Response format

Most items use structured factual response options (e.g., specific time bands for detection and containment, specific role titles for reporting lines, specific certification bodies) rather than Likert agreement scales. Where a Likert scale is used — specifically for questions about board engagement quality that resist factual operationalization — this is noted in [docs/question-rationale.md](docs/question-rationale.md) with the rationale for the exception.

The instrument has been tested for completion time. Target completion time is 12–18 minutes for a prepared respondent (someone with direct knowledge of the program, not someone estimating on behalf of another function). Surveys that take longer than 20 minutes produce systematically worse data in the security practitioner population, where survey fatigue and time constraints are significant.

## 3. Sampling Approach

### 3.1 Target population

The study targets security and GRC practitioners in mid-market organizations: companies with 250–5,000 employees operating in regulated or security-sensitive industries (financial services, healthcare, manufacturing with OT exposure, professional services, and SaaS/technology). This range is chosen because:

- It is the segment most frequently excluded from vendor-funded benchmarks, which overrepresent enterprise accounts.
- It is large enough to have formal GRC programs but small enough that program quality varies substantially with individual decisions — making it the most analytically interesting range for the research questions.
- It represents the majority of the U.S. commercial base by headcount but a minority of published security research.

### 3.2 Eligible respondents

An eligible respondent is a practitioner with direct operational knowledge of their organization's GRC program: CISO, VP/Director of Security, GRC Lead, vCISO, Head of Compliance, or equivalent. We do not accept responses from marketing or HR functions, vendor employees responding about their customers, or consultants responding about client engagements. Respondents are asked to attest eligibility at intake.

### 3.3 Recruitment

Wave 1 respondents are recruited through practitioner networks: direct outreach to CISO/security communities, LinkedIn practitioner groups, invitation through independent security listservs and Slack communities, and referral from Wave 1 participants (snowball component, bounded to limit homophily bias — see §4). We do not pay for panel access, purchase respondent lists, or recruit through vendor marketing channels.

### 3.4 Target N

**Wave 1 target:** N = 150 complete, eligible responses. This provides adequate power for the primary analyses (see §3.5) while remaining achievable through practitioner network recruitment alone.

**Preliminary release threshold:** N = 50. At N ≥ 50, preliminary findings are published to [findings/wave-01-preliminary.md](findings/wave-01-preliminary.md) with explicit power caveats. This prevents the common failure mode of withholding all findings until an ambitious N is reached and then never publishing.

**Wave 2+ target:** N = 200 per wave, enabling longitudinal replication and subgroup analyses not powered in Wave 1.

### 3.5 Power analysis

The primary hypothesis tests require detecting medium-effect-size differences (d ≈ 0.4–0.5) between subgroups. At N = 150, two-tailed independent-samples tests achieve approximately 0.80 power at α = 0.05 for medium effects. This is standard for exploratory survey research with a hard-to-reach population. Wave 1 is explicitly positioned as a foundation study, not a definitive study — the power level reflects that positioning.

## 4. Bias Mitigation

### 4.1 No vendor sponsorship

HC-Assay accepts no vendor funding, no in-kind sponsorship, no co-branding arrangements, and no access to vendor customer panels in exchange for data sharing or co-authorship rights. This is a structural constraint, not a preference. Vendor-funded benchmarks systematically produce findings that validate the frameworks and product categories their sponsors sell. The HC-Assay competitive asset is the absence of that incentive. Compromising it for recruitment convenience eliminates the study's reason for existence.

### 4.2 Pre-registration

All five research hypotheses (RQ1–RQ5) and their decision rules — including the operationalization of each construct, the test statistic, the threshold for each verdict, and the planned analytic strategy — are pre-registered and cryptographically timestamped before any Wave 1 data collection begins. Pre-registration records are stored in the repository under `study_runner/` and published to the findings directory.

This is honestly described as an adaptive design with pre-registered hypotheses: because this is a survey study (not a controlled experiment), we are not predicting outcomes entirely blind. The questions are theoretically motivated by prior practitioner literature. But the specific operationalizations, decision rules, and analytic strategy are locked before first response, which prevents the failure mode of choosing analysis approaches after seeing the data.

### 4.3 Blind baseline scoring

Survey responses are scored against the pre-registered scoring model ([docs/scoring-model.md](docs/scoring-model.md)) by the hc-assay engine pipeline, which applies the scoring rules mechanically. The researcher does not manually assign scores to individual responses. The scoring logic is public, versioned, and auditable.

### 4.4 Snowball bound

The snowball recruitment component (respondent referrals) is bounded: no more than 30% of Wave 1 responses may originate from referral chains traceable to a single seed respondent. This limits the homophily bias that unconstrained snowball sampling introduces (networks of similar practitioners referring each other to a survey).

### 4.5 Non-response analysis

At wave close, we will publish a non-response analysis comparing early and late respondents across observable characteristics (industry sector, org size band, reported maturity level). Systematic differences between early and late respondents are the closest available proxy for response bias in practitioner survey populations.

## 5. Wave Structure

### Wave 1 — Foundation/Baseline

**Purpose:** Establish baseline distributions across all five scoring dimensions; generate preliminary findings on all five research questions; calibrate the instrument for Wave 2.

**Collection period:** Opens 2026-06-18; closes at N = 150 complete responses or 90 days from open, whichever comes first.

**Output:** Preliminary findings at N ≥ 50; final Wave 1 findings at wave close; full anonymized dataset published to [data/](data/).

**Instrument stability:** The Wave 1 instrument is frozen at collection open. No items may be added, removed, or modified during collection. Changes identified during Wave 1 are logged and incorporated into the Wave 2 instrument revision.

### Wave 2+ — Longitudinal Replication

**Purpose:** Test longitudinal stability of findings; extend to subgroup analyses not powered in Wave 1; replicate or refute Wave 1 primary findings.

**Cadence:** Annual, aligned to the calendar year following Wave 1 close.

**Instrument revision protocol:** Each inter-wave period includes a formal instrument revision process. Items that did not perform (low variance, floor/ceiling effects, high non-response) are revised or replaced. New items may be added to address hypotheses surfaced from Wave 1 findings. At least 70% of core items must be retained unmodified to enable longitudinal comparison.

## 6. Validity and Reproducibility Commitments

### 6.1 Published anonymized data

The complete anonymized response dataset for each wave is published to this repository under [data/](data/) at wave close. The data schema is specified in [data/README.md](data/README.md). Any researcher with a copy of the data and the published scoring code can reconstruct the findings independently.

### 6.2 Versioned methodology

This document is version-controlled. Every change to the methodology after Wave 1 opens is recorded with a version number, an effective date, and a description of the change. Methodology changes do not apply retroactively to waves that have already begun collection.

### 6.3 Hostile-reviewer reproducibility

Findings are produced by committing to the following: a hostile reviewer — one who assumes the worst about our choices — must be able to reproduce the result from the published data, published code, and published methodology alone. If they cannot, the finding is not published. This is a higher bar than most practitioner research and a lower bar than a controlled experiment. We state it explicitly so readers know what they are getting.

### 6.4 Limitations disclosed

Each wave's findings document includes a section on limitations that is written before the data is analyzed — a pre-committed list of what this instrument cannot measure, where it is likely to be underpowered, and where the population is likely to be non-representative. These limitations are not added after the fact to explain inconvenient results.

## 7. Ethical Commitments

### 7.1 Respondent anonymity

No response is published in a form that could identify an individual respondent or their organization. The published dataset contains no names, email addresses, job titles, company names, geographic identifiers more specific than region (US/EU/APAC/Other), or any combination of fields that would enable re-identification of an organization with more than 1-in-k probability (k ≥ 5; see §7.3).

### 7.2 No individual disclosure

Respondents are never identified, quoted, or paraphrased in findings — even with consent. The unit of analysis is the aggregate pattern, not the individual respondent.

### 7.3 K-anonymity floor

The published dataset enforces a minimum cell size of 5 for all published subgroup breakdowns. No cross-tabulation or subgroup finding is published if the underlying cell contains fewer than 5 respondents. Where k-anonymity cannot be achieved for a planned analysis, the analysis is deferred to a subsequent wave with higher N, noted as such.

### 7.4 Informed consent

All respondents receive a complete description of the study purpose, data handling practices, and publication plan before completing the survey. Participation is voluntary. Respondents may withdraw at any point before wave close, at which point their response is removed from the dataset.

### 7.5 No secondary use

Data collected for this study will not be sold, shared with vendors, used for recruitment, or applied to any purpose other than the published research questions. This is a structural commitment enforced by the single-operator, no-vendor-sponsorship operating model.
